# Worked Example: expense-management SaaS, from scratch (eval 1)

This is the anti-generic proof artifact: one full, filled-in strategy so the standard is unmistakable. It shows what "commit to named things" looks like end to end. Scenario (eval 1): B2B SaaS selling expense-management software to finance teams at 50 to 500 employee companies, no blog yet, starting from scratch.

Everything here obeys the PRIME rules (no em/en dash, no emoji) and the NON-NEGOTIABLE rules (named pillars with theses, scored pieces, dated calendar that fits the budget, no invented volumes). Numbers in the score table are illustrative judgment scores (0 to 10 per factor), NOT search volumes; the `[needs keyword data]` tags mark where real Ahrefs/GSC data would replace a relevance estimate. Do not copy these as facts, copy the SHAPE.

---

## 0. Intake summary

- Product: expense-management software (capture, approvals, reconciliation, reporting) for finance teams.
- ICP: finance teams at 50 to 500 person companies.
- Goal: start a blog from zero, build search-driven inbound + authority.
- Resource budget: **1 content marketer at 2 posts/week** (stated for the calendar; the cadence-reality gate uses this).
- `.agents/product-marketing-context.md`: not present, proceeded on the intake above.

## 1. Audience map (Phase 2)

```
Segment 1: FP&A lead / Finance Manager at a 50-500 person company (the practitioner).
Job to be done: cut month-end close from 10 days to 5, with numbers that tie out.
Pains: manual reconciliation across spreadsheets, chasing receipts, no audit trail, close is a monthly fire drill.
Vocabulary: "the close", "month-end is a fire drill", "tie out the numbers", "reconcile", "receipts chase".

Segment 2: CFO / VP Finance (the economic buyer).
Job to be done: control company spend without slowing the business down.
Pains: no real-time spend visibility, surprise overspend, policy that nobody follows.
Vocabulary: "spend visibility", "burn", "runway", "control without bureaucracy", "policy compliance".
```

Buyer-stage skew: Segment 1 lives in awareness + implementation (they search how-to and templates); Segment 2 lives in consideration + decision (they evaluate and buy). The calendar mix must serve both.

## 2. Content pillars (Phase 3): 4 named, each with an ownership thesis

Generated with the four lenses, validated against the 4-gate test, named with a thesis. Each passed all four gates (product-aligned, audience-cares, has demand or `[needs validation]`, broad enough for >=5 subtopics).

```
### Pillar 1: Close the Month Faster
Ownership thesis: we own the month-end close pain because our product sits on the exact data that shows where a close stalls, so we can teach the fix with evidence competitors do not have.
Why us: anonymized close-cycle timing across our customer base, plus the reconciliation workflow itself.
Subtopics: close checklist, reconciliation, receipt capture, accruals, close automation, close KPIs, faster tie-out.

### Pillar 2: Spend Control Without the Bureaucracy
Ownership thesis: we own the "control spend without slowing the team" tension, the CFO wants visibility, the team wants speed, and our product is the argument that you do not have to choose.
Why us: real policy-compliance and approval-cycle data + customer stories of teams that kept speed while adding control.
Subtopics: expense policy, approval workflows, corporate card vs reimbursement, spend visibility, policy compliance, cost of manual expenses.

### Pillar 3: The Finance Team Tech Stack
Ownership thesis: we own the "how a modern finance team is tooled" category conversation, which is where buyers in consideration and decision live, and where our comparisons and integrations win the click.
Why us: real integration surface (ERP, accounting, cards) and honest category comparisons.
Subtopics: category definition, software comparisons, competitor alternatives, integrations, pricing, buying guides.

### Pillar 4: Audit-Ready by Default
Ownership thesis: we own "audit-ready without extra work", the compliance pain that a growing finance team feels right before a raise or an audit, and our audit trail is the product proof.
Why us: the audit-trail feature + compliance-oriented customer stories.
Subtopics: audit trail, expense fraud, SOX-lite controls, receipt retention, approval logs, audit prep.
```

None was a bare category label ("Finance", "Expenses"). Each names a claim and why this brand can own it. That is the difference between a strategy and a table of contents.

## 4. Editorial calendar (Phase 9): the arithmetic, then the dated plan

**Cadence-reality gate:** 1 content marketer x 2 posts/week x 4 weeks = **8 slots**. There are 12 candidates, so 4 must be deferred. Schedule the top 8 by score, THEN adjust for buyer-stage balance.

**Balance check on the pure-score top 8** (ranks 1 to 8): stages come out 1 awareness, 3 consideration, 4 implementation, **0 decision**. A plan with no decision-stage content leaves the bottom of the funnel unserved. So make ONE deliberate swap:

- DEFER rank 8 "How to build an audit trail" (7.3, implementation, we already have 3 implementation pieces).
- PROMOTE rank 10 "How [Product] pricing works" (7.2, decision) into the 8th slot, to cover the decision stage.

Net cost of the swap: 0.1 total score, bought a whole funnel stage. That is the judgment the score table informs but does not make for you.

**Deferred backlog (with scores, first to pull next month):** audit trail (7.3), real cost of manual (7.3), what is spend management (6.9), behind the scenes (6.1).

## 5. Handoff: one piece brief (where /content-strategy STOPS)

The strategy does not write the post. It hands /copywriting a brief per piece. Example for the first scheduled piece:

```
Brief for /copywriting
Title (working): How to cut month-end close from 10 days to 5
Pillar: Close the Month Faster
Type: how-to (searchable)
Buyer stage: awareness
Target keyword: month end close process [needs keyword data, confirm volume/difficulty]
Audience: Segment 1 (FP&A lead, "month-end is a fire drill")
Angle: the specific 5 bottlenecks that add days to a close, and the fix for each, backed by our close-cycle data.
Must include: a downloadable close checklist CTA (links to the rank-4 template piece), one real before/after.
Internal links: up to nothing yet (this is early), down to the reconciliation template + close checklist once live.
Voice: professional finance-practitioner. If this were Christopher's own brand voice, /copywriting owns the register.
Do NOT: invent a "close is 40% faster" stat; keep claims to what the data supports.
```

That is the boundary in action: content-strategy commits the WHAT and the WHY; /copywriting writes the piece and gates it; if this were a launch-tied flagship it would route to /launch-strategy.

## 6. If this were an Indonesia-first product (the additive lens, not the default)

The example above is a generic (English) B2B SaaS to show the skill works for any client. If the SAME product targeted Indonesia (like Pulse), the additive lens (rule 9, `references/ideation-sources.md`) would re-point it, WITHOUT changing the frameworks:

- **Language split:** pillars 1 and 4 (practitioner how-to + implementation) go id-first (the FP&A lead searches "cara mempercepat tutup buku"); pillar 3 (category comparisons) stays id with English product terms; a category-defining thought-leadership pillar could be en. Tag each row `lang: id | en | both`.
- **Ideation source swap:** source the pains from local finance/UMKM communities (Kaskus, r/finansial, local accounting Telegram groups), not Indie Hackers.
- **Distribution:** WA-forwardable checklists outperform long essays; plan channels from `references/distribution-reality.md`, and remember posting mechanics defer to /agent-browser.
- **Transcreation, not translation:** a `both` piece is a transcreated id+en pair via /copywriting, never a machine translation.

The 4 pillars, the 4-gate test, the 40/30/20/10 scoring, the cadence-reality gate, and the dated calendar are identical. The lens changes the language and the sources, not the machinery.
